Overview

The power of the unaided mind is highly overrated… The real powers come from devising external aids that enhance cognitive abilities. —Donald Norman

Through this project I wanted to extend the resources that are available to teach concepts of programming to children by providing them with a theoretical understanding of elementary algorithms. Communicating the theoretical concepts of an algorithm for school children provided an interesting research and design challenge, which was explored through task-based learning methods and visualizations.

Role

I was the sole researcher working on the project. The evaluation of the system to understand the effectiveness of the system is, however, being undertaken by a team consisting of S. Madhuri and myself.

Literature Research

Literature research pertaining to AVs primarily discussed:

Effectiveness of Algorithm Visualization systems

AVs where students were required to answer questions about the visualization being viewed (Responding), or even higher levels of engagement and interactions (Changing, Constructing, Presenting) were found to be significantly more effective than AVs where only Viewing was required.

Algorithm Visualization use in classrooms

It was found that a lot of instructors believe that AVs would improve CS learning and that the use of AVs for instruction, however is not widespread.

Salient Features of effective Algorithm Visualizations

It was found that the presence of these features made AVs more effective:

  • Ability to control the pace of the visualization
  • Visualizing a good data set which covers all the special cases
  • Focus on the logical steps of an algorithm
  • Feedback of the visualization through textual explanation or pseudo-code
  • Depicting state changes on the data set & corresponding steps in the algorithm

Analysis & Insights

Through the review of literature and the subsequent analysis, I found the following gaps in the field of AVs. These gaps provided me with plenty of scope for undertaking further research and opportunity to design an intervention.

Lack of effective visualization as most AVs are at the Viewing level

Disproportionate share of AVs on fundamental algorithms

Lack of a complete catalogue for AVs

No AVs target students below CS undergraduate level

The analysis revealed numerous possible directions for me to proceed. Hoever, based on my experience and my general interests, I decided to address the gap of the absence of tools which target students at the school level. as I felt that this would be an interesting design challenge as well as provide me with the best platform to apply my knowledge acquired during my years as an undergraduate.

Problem Statement

How to enable the learning of concepts of elementary sorting algorithms for Children in Grades VII – X , with little or no programming proficiency, by focusing on the logical and working steps involved in the algorithms?

Instructional Goals

Instructional Content

The concept of fundamental comparison sorts presented by ensuring the instruction:

  • centred around real-world tasks or problems,
  • involved recall of prior knowledge for learning, and
  • involved application on such real-world tasks or problems for learning.

Delivery

A constructivist approach has been adopted for delivery:

  • First, allowing the user to explore the problem space by presenting her with the problem,
  • Followed by, using visualizations to acquaint the user with the subject matter i.e. sorting algorithms,
  • Finally, the user needs to perform a reconstruction of the presented subject matter by completing the task again.

Environment

A web-based learning platform was proposed as it provided the following advantages:

  • Ease of accessibility to internet through a computer, and
  • Greater number and flexibility of interactions possible through a computer

Ideation

Most AVs discussed in literature were very abstract. Due to our target audience being school children, a visualization should also be able to communicate the need for using an algorithm through the use of context based visualizations.
Thus, I identified different real world applications of sorting algorithms.

For the design of the system, we used the instructional design model of Merril's First Principles of Instruction as guidelines. These principles determined the content and how it should be presented for effective instruction.

I decided to choose the task of sorting weights using balance scales to present the users with the sorting challenge as it provides a natural analogy for the chlidren (The demography of the children taken into consideration were Indian students).

Work Flow

Design & Prototyping

The design attempted to provide the users with a simple, clean and intuitive interface which communicates clearly the goals of the system. I developed an initial web-based prototype and performed usability tests of the interface by the appropriate audience.

I refined the initial interfaces based on feedback received from the usability tests to create the final prototype.

Evaluation

We are currently testing the system on a group of children from Std VIII to X to understand it's effectiveness. This group will be allowed to interact with the system (first the task and then the explanation using the visualization). They will then attempt the task again and explain their actions and subsequently will be presented to some written problems. The success of the system will be judged based on their performance on these tasks and problems. This will be compared to a control group of children who will be explained about the same sorting algorithms on a blackboard and will be presented with the same task and questions to measure their performance.
