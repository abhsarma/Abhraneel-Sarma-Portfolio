Master's Thesis

Revisiting Storytelling in Information Visualization: Does it have any effect on recall and engagement?

Advisor: Prof. Matthey Kay

Overview

(Ongoing Project)

There are different strategies that are adopted in the visualization design. These are the use of interactivity, narrative, a combination of the interactivity and narrative, or static (no-interactivity or non-narrative). Although each of these approaches will allow the viewer to engage with the data in different ways, a narrative based approach or a combination of the interactive and narrative visualization approach, may allow the viewer to better recall the meaningful aspects of the data which were highlighted through the story - such as trends and patterns in the data, and also affect how users interact with the data.

The purpose of this study is to measure the effect of using different visualization strategies on (1) recall, operationalized through two variables: interactivity (static or interactive) and narrative (non-narrative or narrative); and (2) engagement, measured through time-spent and number of clicks to manipulate the data presented in the visualization. We aim to measure differences in recall of visualization content and interaction with the visualization across the four combinations of these variables (static+non-narrative, static+narrative, interactive+non-narrative, interactive+narrative).

01. Methodology

Research Questions

I initially did a comprehensive literature review of prior research on Storytelling in Information Visualization. This helped me identify possible areas for research. I found the work by Boy et. al. (2015) very interesting. However, I found that the conclusions of the result were constrained as it only analysed engagement.

My goal was to build on this study and I came up with the following set of research questions:
What is the effect of narrative and interactivity on viewer recall of the data presented in the visualization?
What is the effect of engagement (measured through the duration of time spent on visualization and number of meaningful interactions) on recall?

Study Design

I adapted 4 professionally designed visualizations1 and redesigned them to fit into each of the four visualization strategies (interactive, narrative, a combination of the interactive and narrative, or static). Thus there were 16 unique combinations of condition and visualization. I then created a set of True or False questions which asked about the data presented, to test participant recall. The questions were created in such a way that some of them were better suited to be answered in the narrative condition, whereas the others were better suited to the interactive condition

Study Methodology

We first pilot tested the survey to see the percentage of correct responses - this helped us identify poorly worded questions as well as get preliminary data on the number of questions an average respondent will get correct (and determine how much to pay workers on MTurk). We recruited 400 participants on Amazon's Mechanical Turk in a completely between subjects design - each participant saw one visualization, in one condition. On average each participant was paid $2.1.

Data Analysis

To analyse the data I fit a bayesian mixed-effects logistic regression model with the number of questions answered correctly in each condition as the dependent variable and the condition that the participants were placed into as the independent variable. We took into account random-effects due to varying ability of the participants, differences in the difficulty of the questions (within and across visualizations), and effects of the visualization itself.

Currently we are still in the data analysis stage.
